Introduction

At the beginning of 2021, just after the announcement of another lockdown in the UK, I decided I needed a new side-project to work on. As a way to teach myself about RL, a couple of years ago I built a simulator and trained an agent to play the card game "Big 2", a reasonably complex four-player game of imperfect information. This worked surprisingly well, and having spent the intervening years carrying out research in RL (as a postdoc and now as a research scientist at Rowden Technologies) I thought that I'd like to try something more challenging still. In recent years RL has attracted a lot of attention due to the extremely impressive results that have been achieved in applying RL to games such as Go, DOTA 2 and Starcraft 2. However, all of these projects required huge teams of world class researchers/software engineers and tens of millions of dollars worth of compute power - meaning that attempting to solve anything of comparable complexity as an individual researcher working on a beefy (but still single) desktop PC is pretty unlikely to get very far! On the other hand, so much RL research these days is carried out on relatively simple environments (at least in terms of the structure of their state/action spaces), which can sometimes get a bit boring in my view, and so I definitely wanted to look at something different.

I ended up deciding that an interesting game to try would be "Settlers of Catan" - a hugely popular board game that has sold over 35 million copies worldwide and which I personally enjoy playing with friends. This is a game with a complex state/action space, as well as being a 4-player game with some hidden information, and quite substantially more complex than Big 2. This makes it a serious challenge, however it is still significantly more accessible than an RTS like Starcraft 2, and so I thought it might be possible to make some progress. This article/post is a detailed dive into the approach I took and the results I was able to achieve.
